Building Resilient Supply Chains in a Fractured Geopolitical Landscape

Thomas Brennan
Operations & Global Trade Analyst · 7 min read

The global supply chain model that underpinned three decades of economic expansion was built on a set of assumptions that the last five years have systematically dismantled. Just-in-time manufacturing required stable geopolitics. Single-source supplier relationships required predictable logistics. Geographic concentration — particularly in East Asia — required that efficiency and resilience could be optimised simultaneously. None of those conditions reliably hold anymore.

What has replaced them is a fractured landscape in which companies must make deliberate, often expensive trade-offs between efficiency and resilience. The organisations navigating this most effectively are those that made the decision early to treat supply chain resilience as a strategic priority rather than an operational cost to be minimised.

Editorial Note: This article reflects current industry analysis and research. Supply chain decisions involve complex trade-offs specific to each organisation's sector, geography, and risk profile. Professional operational and legal advice should be sought before making material supply chain changes.

The Anatomy of Fragility

When the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted global supply chains in 2020 and 2021, it exposed vulnerabilities that had been building for decades. Single-source dependencies, inadequate inventory buffers, and opaque multi-tier supplier networks left companies unable to respond when conditions changed suddenly.

The years since have added further stressors: the Russia-Ukraine conflict disrupting energy and grain supplies; US-China trade tensions fragmenting technology supply chains; and a series of extreme weather events underscoring the physical risks embedded in geographically concentrated production.

"Supply chain resilience is not a defensive posture — it is a competitive advantage. The companies that built redundancy before they needed it are now outperforming those that didn't."— Thomas Brennan, Meridian Insights

The Nearshoring Imperative

Nearshoring — relocating production or sourcing to geographically proximate countries — has moved from a niche risk mitigation strategy to a mainstream operational shift. For US companies, this has meant accelerating sourcing from Mexico, Central America, and the Caribbean Basin. For European companies, Morocco, Poland, Romania, and Turkey have become increasingly important manufacturing and sourcing hubs.

The economics of nearshoring are nuanced. Labour cost differentials have narrowed as emerging market wages have risen. Transportation and logistics savings, reduced inventory holding costs, faster response times, and lower geopolitical risk can more than offset the remaining cost differential — but this calculation must be made rigorously, and it varies significantly by sector.

Nearshoring and supplier diversification are reshaping global manufacturing geography.

Supplier Diversification: Beyond the Obvious

The instinctive response to supply chain concentration risk is supplier diversification — and it is the right instinct. But companies frequently implement it superficially, adding a second or third supplier from the same geographic region, or in the same geopolitical bloc, as their primary source. This creates the appearance of diversification without the substance.

True diversification requires mapping concentration risk across multiple dimensions simultaneously: geography, geopolitical alignment, infrastructure dependency, currency exposure, and climate vulnerability. A supplier network that looks diversified on a map may share critical single points of failure in shipping routes, port capacity, or power infrastructure.

The Dual-Source Discipline

One practical framework gaining traction among operations leaders is the dual-source discipline: for every critical component or material, maintain at least two qualified, audited suppliers from different geopolitical regions, with the capability to supply at full volume with reasonable lead time. This is not merely about having backup suppliers on paper — it requires active relationship management, regular order volumes sufficient to maintain production readiness, and investment in supplier development.

Technology as a Resilience Enabler

Supply chain visibility — understanding in real time where materials are, what inventory levels exist at each tier, and where bottlenecks are forming — has historically been poor, even in sophisticated organisations. AI-powered supply chain platforms are changing this, aggregating data from across supplier networks, logistics providers, and customs systems to provide operational intelligence that was previously unavailable.

Companies investing in supply chain technology are not merely improving operational efficiency — they are building the sensing capability required to identify disruptions early enough to respond. In a fragmented geopolitical environment, early warning is often the difference between a managed disruption and a crisis.

The CFO's Role in Supply Chain Resilience

Supply chain resilience has historically been seen as an operations function. The disruptions of recent years have demonstrated that it is a financial risk management issue of the first order. Chief Financial Officers who are not actively engaged in understanding their organisation's supply chain risk profile — and investing in its mitigation — are leaving material value at risk.

The calculus is straightforward: the cost of building resilience is predictable and finite. The cost of a major supply chain disruption — measured in lost revenue, emergency procurement premiums, customer attrition, and reputational damage — is far less predictable and potentially far larger.
